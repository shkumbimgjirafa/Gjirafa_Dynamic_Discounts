1What this advisor is for

Elasticity answers the one question none of the other advisors can:

"When we move this product's price, does demand actually respond — and if it does, what single price earns us the most profit?"

Sell-through reasons about inventory clearing; margin-tier about headroom; dead-stock about frozen stock. None of them looks at the shape of the demand curve. Elasticity does — and only elasticity. It owns one orthogonal lane: the price-responsiveness lane. It reads a per-SKU elasticity coefficient that was fitted weekly from years of real transaction history, and, only when it is statistically confident demand is genuinely elastic, votes the textbook profit-maximising price.

It is deliberately conservative about when it speaks. A fitted coefficient is a noisy estimate; acting on a wrong one can swing a price hard in the wrong direction. So elasticity stays silent on inelastic SKUs, unfitted SKUs, cost-less SKUs, and — crucially — on noisy fits that only look elastic (§8). When it does vote, weight 80 makes it one of the loudest voices in the blend, so the bar to speak is high.

2The demand model: constant elasticity

"Price elasticity of demand" E is the percentage change in units sold for a 1 % change in price. If a 1 % price rise drops volume by 2 %, then E = −2. Because demand falls as price rises, E is always negative. We model demand with the constant-elasticity form — the standard workhorse, because it makes that "% for %" relationship hold at every price:

(1) Q(P)  =  A·P E  ,   E < 0

Q is units, P is price, A is a scale factor (overall popularity). The magnitude of E is the whole story:

RegionCoefficientMeaningWhat it implies for price
Inelastic−1 < E < 0Volume barely reacts to priceRevenue rises as you raise price → no interior profit peak. Algo 5 silent
Unit-elasticE = −1% drop in volume = % rise in priceRevenue flat; profit formula degenerate. Algo 5 silent
ElasticE < −1Volume reacts more than proportionallyA finite profit-max price P* exists (§6). Algo 5 acts

The reason we use this form is that it becomes a straight line in log space. Take logs of (1):

(2) ln Q  =  ln A  +  E·ln P

This is y = intercept + slope·x with x = ln P, y = ln Q. The slope is the elasticity E. So fitting elasticity reduces to fitting a single straight line through (ln price, ln units) points — the same curves above, but now perfectly straight, their slope equal to E:

3Fitting it: log-log ordinary least squares

We never observe the true curve — only scattered weekly observations of "this many units sold at roughly this price." We fit the line (2) by ordinary least squares (OLS): choose the slope and intercept that minimise the sum of squared vertical gaps between the data and the line. The closed-form solution (OlsRegression.cs) for n points is:

(3) Ê  =  slope  =  Σ(xi−x̄)(yi−ȳ) Σ(xi−x̄)2  =  SxySxx  ,   intercept  =  ȳ − Ê·x̄

We also need to know how trustworthy the slope is. Two diagnostics do that work:

(4) R2  =  Sxy 2Sxx·Syy        SE(Ê)  =  √ SSE / (n−2)Sxx  ,   SSE = Syy − Ê·Sxy

where Sxx, Syy, Sxy are the centred sums of squares and cross-products. R2 (0–1) says how much of the variation in log-units the price line explains; SE is the standard error of the slope — the ± band on our estimate of E, and the linchpin of the confidence gate (§8). With fewer than 3 points there are no residual degrees of freedom, so SE is ∞ and the fit is never trusted.

4The data pipeline (how the points are built)

The fit runs weekly per (layer, SKU) in ElasticityFitService, over a trailing 365-day window (one full seasonal cycle of price points). The raw fact table is SR_ProductsData (order lines). The expensive aggregation is done set-based in SQL (SqlElasticitySourceReader) so only one tiny row per SKU ever comes back to C#:

5The usability gate (is the fit even real?)

A slope can be computed from almost anything; that doesn't make it meaningful. Before a coefficient is ever stored as IsUsable, ElasticityGate.IsUsable requires all of these — a mix of fit-quality and economic-plausibility tests:

GateThresholdRejects…
Enough observationsN ≥ 8 weeksSKUs with too little history to fit a line.
Enough distinct prices≥ 4SKUs whose price never really moved — nothing to regress against.
Real price spreadmax/min ≥ 1.05Prices that wobbled <5 % — too little leverage to identify a slope.
Fit explains variationR² ≥ 0.20Scatter with no real price→volume relationship.
Right sign & not noise−8 < E < −0.05Positive slopes (wrong sign), near-zero noise, and absurdly steep collinear artifacts.

A usable coefficient may still be inelastic (e.g. E=−0.5). The gate keeps it for analysis — but Algorithm 5 acts only on the elastic ones (§6), and even then only the confident ones (§8). "Usable" and "acted on" are two different bars.

6From a slope to a price: profit maximisation

A confident elastic slope tells us the demand curve's shape. Now we pick the price on it that earns the most profit. Per-unit profit is (P − c) where c is cost (PPTCV, the all-in VAT-inclusive landed cost), and we sell Q(P) units, so total profit is:

(5) π(P)  =  (P − c)·Q(P)  =  (P − c)·A·P E

Differentiate and set to zero. Factor out A·PE−1:

(6) dπdP  =  A·P E−1 [ P(1+E) − E c ]  =  0   ⇒   P(1+E) = E c

Solve for P and you get the profit-maximising price the algorithm votes:

(7) P*  =  c · EE+1  =  c · m(E)

The factor m(E) = E/(E+1) is the optimal markup over cost. For elastic demand (E < −1) both E and E+1 are negative, so m > 1 and P* is a sensible price above cost. Two things to notice:

  • More elastic → price nearer cost. As E→−∞, m→1, so P*→cost: very price-sensitive demand is best served by a thin margin and high volume.
  • This is a maximum only for E<−1. For inelastic demand (−1<E<0), equation (5) is monotonically increasing in P — there's no interior peak, profit just keeps rising with price. That's the formal reason elasticity abstains on inelastic SKUs and leaves them to the margin-tier advisor and the anchor cap.

7The markup blow-up near E = −1

Equation (7) hides a trap. As E approaches −1 from below, the denominator E+1 approaches 0, so the markup m(E) explodes toward infinity:

(8) m(−2) = −2−1 = 2×   |   m(−1.5) = −1.5−0.5 = 3×   |   m(−1.18) = −1.18−0.18 ≈ 6.6×   |   m(−1.05) ≈ 21×

8The confidence gate (when are we allowed to act?)

Because of the blow-up, it isn't enough that the point estimate Ê is below −1. We require that we're statistically confident the true E is below −1 — even the optimistic (closest-to-zero) end of a one-sided 95 % confidence interval must clear −1 (ElasticityGate.IsConfidentlyElastic):

(9) Ê  +  z·SE(Ê)  ≤  −1  ,   z = 1.645  (one-sided 95 %)

In words: take the fitted slope, walk 1.645 standard errors toward zero (the least-elastic the data plausibly allows), and only act if you're still at or below −1. A non-finite SE (too few points) can never satisfy this, so it's never confident.

Two distinct elastic bars. IsElastic(E) = E < −1 (the point estimate) decides what's labelled elastic for analysis. IsConfidentlyElastic(E, SE) = equation (9) decides what Algorithm 5 is allowed to act on. The second is strictly stronger — it's what saves us from the §7 blow-up.

9The vote it casts

Once a SKU passes both the usability gate (§5) and the confidence gate (§8), the algorithm emits exactly one vote:

1

Suggested price

optimal = min( cost × E/(E+1), anchor ) — the profit-max price P* from (7), capped at the anchor (FinalPrice). The engine never proposes above the reference price, and the cap also tames the residual markup near E=−1 that survives the confidence gate.
2

Confidence

confidence = min( 0.75, 0.45 + (|E| − 1)/4 ) — rises with how elastic the SKU is, from 0.45 at the unit-elastic edge up to a 0.75 ceiling (e.g. E=−2 → 0.70; E=−3 and beyond → 0.75). More elastic = more conviction.
3

Reason code

Always ELASTIC_OPTIMAL, with text naming the fitted E, its SE, the markup, the proposed price and the direction (↑/↓) from today's price.

10From a vote to a price

Elasticity produces one vote. Like every advisor it is then blended, clamped and rounded:

1

Blend

Weighted average of all advisors' votes, each vote's pull = band weight × confidence. At weight 80 and confidence up to 0.75, elasticity's effective pull (up to ~60) is the heaviest in the engine — when it speaks, it usually leads the blend.
2

Clamp to [margin floor, anchor]

The blended price is bounded below by the band's margin-floor price cost ÷ (1 − floor%) and above by the anchor. Elasticity gets no tunnel exception — unlike dead stock, a profit-max price that lands below the floor is clamped right back up to it.
3

Round

Snap to the band's psychological grid (e.g. .99), but only if rounding stays inside the guardrails.

11Worked examples

Three illustrative SKUs (Band 2, €10–50, 13 % margin floor) that exercise the three outcomes: a clean elastic cut, the confidence gate firing, and the anchor cap. All arithmetic is shown.

Example A — confidently elastic, profit-max cuts the price

FactValue
Cost (PPTCV) / Anchor / Current€12.00 / €34.99 / €29.99
Fitted E ± SE−2.40 ± 0.50
Band margin floor13% (→ €13.79)

The algorithm's reasoning

  • Confidence gate: −2.40 + 1.645·0.50 = −1.58 ≤ −1 → confident ✓.
  • Markup: E/(E+1) = −2.40 / −1.40 = ×1.714.
  • P*: 12.00 × 1.714 = €20.57; below the €34.99 anchor, so no cap → optimal €20.57.
  • Confidence value: min(0.75, 0.45 + (2.40−1)/4) = min(0.75, 0.80) = 0.75; arrow ↓ from €29.99.

The rest of the chain

  • Lone voter → raw blended price = €20.57.
  • Margin floor = 12.00 ÷ 0.87 = €13.79; €20.57 sits inside [13.79, 34.99] → no clamp.
  • Rounding (band 2 .99, €1 grid): candidates 19.99 / 20.99 → 20.99 is closer.
  • Final = €20.99 (−30.0% vs €29.99). Margin there ≈ 42.8% — fat, by design: elastic demand rewards a lower price with enough extra volume to maximise total profit.

Read: the textbook case. A genuinely price-responsive SKU is being sold too high; elasticity pulls it down to the profit peak and, as the heaviest voter, the blend follows.

  • Lone voter → raw = €34.99 (the cap). Margin floor = 30 ÷ 0.87 = €34.48; €34.99 inside [34.48, 34.99].

Read: the anchor cap as the second safety rail. Even a clean, confident fit can imply a price above the reference; the engine never proposes increases beyond the anchor, so P* is trimmed to it.

12Gotchas & things to remember

  • The slope is the elasticity — that's the whole reason we regress in log-log space (eq. 2). No transformation needed afterwards.
  • "Usable" ≠ "acted on." The §5 gate stores well-fit coefficients including inelastic ones; Algorithm 5 acts only on the confidently elastic subset (§8).
  • Confidence is about the interval, not the point. A point estimate below −1 is not enough — E + 1.645·SE ≤ −1 must hold, or the near-unit markup blow-up (§7) makes the vote reckless.
  • No SE → never acts. Fewer than 3 points gives an infinite standard error; the confidence gate treats that as never-confident.
  • P* is capped at the anchor and clamped to the margin floor. Elasticity gets no tunnel — only dead stock may pierce the floor.
  • Realised, volume-weighted, VAT-inclusive price drives the fit (Σ BrutoPrice ÷ Σ Qty per week), and cost is PPTCV (also VAT-inclusive) — so P* is already a shelf price, no VAT step.
  • Weekly buckets, 365-day window, refit weekly. Intermittent daily demand is denoised into weeks; a year captures a full seasonal cycle of price points.
  • Inelastic SKUs have no interior profit peak — that's the formal reason elasticity abstains on them rather than a heuristic choice.
